Historical Context

The Sine-Gordon equation (SGE) was originally discovered in the 19th century in the study of surfaces of constant negative curvature. It describes systems where the restoring force is proportional to the sine of the displacement — fundamentally a pendulum chain.

Continuum Limit ∂²φ/∂t² − ∂²φ/∂x² + sin(φ) = 0

The Discrete Model

In this laboratory, we explore the discrete Sine-Gordon system. Instead of a continuous field, we simulate N coupled pendulums. The result is a rich system supporting solitons — stable localized waves that maintain their shape as they travel.

  • Topology: The spatial arrangement (Linear, Circular, or Lemniscate). Circular mode uses periodic boundary conditions.
  • Kinks: Topological twists of ±2π. They are "topological" because they cannot be removed by local deformations.
  • Lorentz Contraction: Because the SGE is Lorentz-invariant in its continuous form, solitons exhibit relativistic effects.

Video Export & Speed

Use the Video Record button to capture the simulation window. The capture happens frame-by-frame after you stop recording, ensuring perfect quality regardless of your computer's real-time performance.

  • Increasing Playback Speed: To make the final video play faster, increase the Export FPS dropdown. A higher frame rate (e.g., 120 or 240 fps) will play through the same number of frames more quickly.
  • Frame Integrity: All captured frames are preserved regardless of target FPS. The simulation's internal time-step determines the physical duration.
  • Format Support: If MP4 is not supported, the system falls back to WebM.

Topologies

The lab supports three spatial topologies, selected from the bottom bar:

  • Linear — A straight chain of pendulums. Waves reflect off the boundaries. Enable Periodic Wrap to connect the ends.
  • Circular — Pendulums arranged on a circle. Kinks travel endlessly around the ring. The winding number tracks net twists around the loop.
  • Lemniscate — A figure-eight (∞) curve. Available in two forms: Gerono (vertical oscillation) and Bernoulli (coplanar).

Physics Parameters

  • N (Elements) — Number of pendulums in the chain (20–720). More elements = finer spatial resolution.
  • κ (Coupling) — Torsional stiffness between neighbors (1–1000). Higher values make the chain stiffer, increasing wave speed.
  • g (Gravity) — Restoring force strength (0.1–40). Stronger gravity makes kinks narrower and faster.
  • γ (Damping) — Velocity-dependent friction (−0.01 to 0.5). Negative values add energy (anti-damping). At zero, the system is conservative.
  • Δt — Fixed at 0.01. The integration timestep. The semi-implicit Euler method is stable for these parameters.
  • Time Scale — Simulation speed multiplier (0.1×–5×). Affects how many physics steps run per animation frame.

Impulse System

The launch panel (top-right) controls soliton injection. Two independent spots (A and B) can be configured with different colors, positions, modes, and directions.

  • Position — Where on the chain the impulse is applied (shown in degrees around the circle).
  • Mode: Kink — A single 2π twist. Travels in the chosen direction.
  • Mode: Anti — A single −2π twist (antikink).
  • Mode: Breath — A kink-antikink pair injected close together, forming a breather.
  • Mode: Wind — Adds a constant phase gradient around the ring (changes winding number).
  • Sharpness — Controls kink width (0.5–8). Lower values = sharper kinks.
  • Amplitude — Kink strength (0.1–2). At 1.0, the kink spans exactly 2π.
  • Velocity — Initial speed (0.5–15). The Lorentz contraction narrows fast kinks.

Press FIRE to inject both spots simultaneously. The simulation unpauses automatically. Use ▶ Run or the side play button to pause/resume.
